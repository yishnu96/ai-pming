## Key Research Findings for AGI Topic (1.4)

### Core Sources Consulted
1. Wikipedia AGI and Superintelligence entries (authoritative overview)
2. arXiv GPT-4 Technical Report (current capabilities/limitations)
3. Britannica AI overview (foundational definitions)

### Organization Pattern That Works
- Define narrow AI first (with relatable examples like ChatGPT, DALL-E)
- Show the gap to AGI clearly (specialist vs. generalist analogy)
- Define superintelligence separately (AGI vs. ASI distinction)
- Current progress section: what modern systems CAN and CANNOT do (honest, not hyped)
- Timeline section: acknowledge expert disagreement, cite historical prediction failures
- Risks/benefits: balanced, acknowledge uncertainty
- Actionable takeaways for non-tech readers

### Content Rules Discovered
- Use professional/academic examples (bar exam passing as metric)
- Avoid sci-fi speculation; stick to researcher consensus
- Emphasize what we DON'T know (timeline uncertainty, feasibility questions)
- Use relatable analogies (chess grandmaster vs. general intelligence)
- Show limitations of current systems alongside capabilities
- Acknowledge "nobody knows" where appropriate
- Key caveat: 1956 AI researchers predicted 20-year path to AGI; still waiting 70 years later

### Angles That Resonate
1. Current AI is narrow (specialist, not generalist)
2. AGI doesn't exist yet (contrary to hype)
3. Timeline is deeply uncertain (5-500 years; experts disagree)
4. Superintelligence is hypothetical (possible outcome, not guaranteed)
5. Risks and benefits both real (depends on how it's built)

### Content Writer Should Avoid
- Stating timelines as fact (use "may," "could," not "will")
- Sci-fi framing (singularity, AI takeover narrative)
- Certainty where experts are uncertain
- Uncited claims about current AI capabilities
