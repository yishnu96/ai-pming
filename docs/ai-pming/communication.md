A key role of an AI Product Manager is to manage internal stakeholders, acting as a shield for the development team so they can focus on their work. An internal stakeholder is anyone within your organization who has an **Interest** in your product, **Influence** over its decisions, or is **Impacted** by its outcome. A simple test to identify a stakeholder is to ask, "Can this person or group prevent our product from launching?" If the answer is yes, you must manage the relationship carefully.

A core tenet of product management is: "If you don't set expectations, someone else will." This is especially true for AI Product Managers, who must proactively manage the organization's "realistic optimism" to prevent stakeholders from being disappointed when a model isn't perfect or a project takes a long time.

### Three Core Expectations to Set for AI Products

To align the organization with the realities of AI development, you must clearly communicate three key expectations:

* **1. AI Projects Often Fail to Launch:** Explain that AI development is **probabilistic**, not deterministic like traditional engineering. An investment does not guarantee a successful model. To reflect this uncertainty, use cautious language like "possible" and "likely" instead of "will" and "can."
* **2. AI is Not Magic:** Counter the media hype by framing AI as "science, not magic." Set the expectation that achieving human-level accuracy is extremely rare and that AI is not the right solution for every problem.
* **3. AI Products Take Time:** Position AI development as a long-term investment in innovation that requires significant trial and error. It is not a quick fix, and stakeholders should be prepared for longer timelines than with traditional software.

### How to Maintain Accountability

Setting these expectations is not about avoiding responsibility for results. To maintain trust and accountability while being realistic, an AI PM must do two things:

1.  **Educate the Organization:** Continuously teach stakeholders the basics of how AI modeling works to build their understanding.
2.  **Provide Frequent Updates:** Unlike traditional projects that give updates at the end of big stages, AI's long and uncertain development phase requires constant communication. Share updates frequently—when a model's error rate improves, when more data is added, and even when a team has invested time *without* seeing improvement.

This transparency helps reinforce the expectations you have set and gives the organization a clear, realistic view of the team's progress and challenges.

Effective stakeholder management is an intricate dance that ensures key players feel heard and become valuable contributors rather than roadblocks. The process involves five key steps:

* **1. Build a Relationship:** Get to know your stakeholders informally, through one-on-one conversations. Avoid overly formal processes that might give them the impression they have more authority over the product than the user.
* **2. Listen to their Perspective:** Understand their role, expertise, and goals within the company. Your aim is to listen for valuable insights and knowledge they can contribute, not necessarily to adopt all of their ideas.
* **3. Educate them on AI and Data:** This is a critical step for AI PMs. Proactively educate stakeholders on the realities of AI—such as why 100% accuracy is impossible—to set realistic expectations and build understanding.
* **4. Keep them Informed:** Provide regular, high-level updates on the product's development process. This builds trust and ensures there are no surprises when it's time to launch.
* **5. Set Boundaries:** Politely but firmly clarify that while their perspective is highly valued, your ultimate accountability is to the **user**. They need to understand that the final decisions are driven by user needs.

By investing time in your stakeholders and helping them understand the unique characteristics of AI projects, you can navigate internal dynamics successfully and give your product the best possible chance of launching without organizational friction.

As a Product Manager, you are the communication hub between your team, your users, and your stakeholders. The foundation of effective communication is not speaking, but listening. To move beyond simply hearing words and toward true understanding, you must practice **Active Listening**. This technique is about using your speaking time to confirm and clarify what the other person is saying.

### The Steps of Active Listening

Active listening is a skill that can be learned by practicing a few key steps during any conversation:

* **1. Pay Close Attention:** Remove all distractions, such as your laptop or phone. Face the speaker directly, maintain eye contact, and use open body language (e.g., don't cross your arms) to show you are fully engaged.
* **2. Show That You Are Listening:** Use small verbal and non-verbal cues to encourage the speaker to continue. Simple actions like nodding or saying "I see" or "uh-huh" signal that you are following along and interested in what they have to say.
* **3. Confirm What You've Heard:** This is the most critical step. After the speaker makes a point, paraphrase your understanding of it back to them. Use phrases like, "What I'm hearing is..." or "It sounds like..." This confirms you've interpreted their message correctly and gives them a chance to clarify if you haven't.
* **4. Ask Clarifying Questions:** If you are genuinely confused, don't guess. Ask direct questions to get more detail. Use prompts like, "Can you tell me more about...?" or "When you say X, do you mean...?" to show your curiosity and ensure you get the information right.

By mastering active listening, you can gather more accurate information from your team, users, and stakeholders. More importantly, it will build your reputation as a skilled communicator, because people recognize that true communication begins with being heard and understood.

## Storytelling 

As a product manager, you constantly communicate complex information through presentations. The most effective way to make your message memorable is not by leading with facts and figures, but by telling a story. Humans are wired to remember stories, so a great presentation should take your stakeholders, team, and users on a journey.

### The Storytelling Funnel for Presentations

Structure your presentation like a funnel, starting with a broad, relatable message and getting progressively more specific. This structure consists of four key parts:

* **1. The Broad Problem:** Start with the "why." Introduce your **Hero** (the user), their **Goal** (what they want to achieve), and the **Villain** (the obstacle in their way). Use direct user quotes to make the problem feel real and important.
* **2. The Sidekick and the Plot:** Introduce the "how." Your product is the **Sidekick** that helps the hero overcome the villain. Briefly explain how your solution helps the user achieve their goal.
* **3. The Numbers:** Introduce the "what," but save it for near the end of your presentation. This is where you provide the key metrics and data points that prove your product's value. This section should be concise and supportive of the main story.
* **4. The Action:** End with the "now what." Conclude with a single, specific action you want your audience to take, whether it's giving their approval, offering support, or making a decision.

### Tips for a More Impactful Story

To make your presentation even more effective:

* **Keep it Simple:** Be concise. Challenge yourself to tell the story in half the time to ensure you're only including the most critical information.
* **Use Memorable Elements:** Enhance your narrative with strong visuals, direct user quotes, and personal anecdotes.
* **Practice:** Rehearse your presentation multiple times to ensure a smooth and confident delivery.

As a product manager, you are the chief advocate for your product and your team. Mastering the art of storytelling is a crucial skill that allows you to create compelling presentations that resonate with your audience and effectively drive action.

## Meetings

Product managers have some of the busiest calendars in an organization, often filled with inefficient meetings. To avoid the common trap of "having a meeting to discuss the last meeting," it's crucial to adopt a disciplined approach to how you plan, run, and follow up on them.

### A Framework for Effective Meetings

To make your meetings more productive and reduce their overall number, follow this four-part framework:

* **1. Decide if a Meeting is Necessary:** Before accepting or scheduling a meeting, always ask three questions first: "Do I truly need to be here, or can I get a write-up later?", "Can this be accomplished with a quick email or message instead?", and "Does this have to happen *now*?"

* **2. Plan the Meeting:** If a meeting is essential, invite only the people who are absolutely needed to contribute or make a decision. Then, send a proper invitation that clearly states the meeting's **Purpose** (why we're meeting), **Goal** (the desired outcome), **Agenda** (how we'll get there), and includes any **pre-read documents**. The rule is simple: no goal, no meeting.

* **3. Run the Meeting:** As the meeting owner, your job is to be a firm facilitator. Keep the conversation focused on the agenda, steer away from unrelated topics, and drive the group toward the stated goal. Before the meeting ends, ensure you have defined clear **action items** with assigned owners.

* **4. Follow Up After:** After the meeting, send a brief **summary of key takeaways and action items** to a wider group of stakeholders who need to be informed but didn't need to be in the room. This keeps everyone in the loop without bloating the invite list and prevents unnecessary follow-up meetings.

By following this disciplined process, you can reclaim your time, respect the time of your colleagues, and ensure that the meetings you do have are focused, productive, and drive clear outcomes.
