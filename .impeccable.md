## Design Context

### Users
**Primary**: Non-tech professionals aged 25-50 — product managers, marketers, sales leaders, CEOs, teachers, HR professionals, consultants, small business owners. They use apps daily but don't code. They've heard of ChatGPT but feel overwhelmed. They fear being left behind by AI but don't know where to start. They want practical, jargon-free guidance they can apply today.

**Secondary**: College students, MBA candidates, professionals pivoting into tech-adjacent roles. They want foundational AI knowledge to be competitive.

**Context of use**: Reading long-form educational articles on desktop or mobile, usually during work hours or commute. They're learning, not browsing — attention is deliberate but patience is limited. If the interface feels like a textbook or a tech blog, they'll bounce.

**Job to be done**: Understand one AI concept per session and know how to apply it at work. Go from "AI sounds cool" to "I use AI every day."

### Brand Personality
**3 words**: Warm. Bold. Clear.

**Voice**: Like a knowledgeable friend explaining something over coffee — direct, encouraging, never condescending. Every sentence earns its place.

**Emotional goals**: The interface should evoke **confidence** ("I can learn this"), **calm** ("this isn't overwhelming"), and **quiet delight** ("this site feels different — in a good way").

### Aesthetic Direction

**Visual tone**: Neobrutalist reading theme — bold and physical at the structural level (hard shadows, thick borders, sharp corners, press interactions), but warm and comfortable at the reading level (cream backgrounds, generous spacing, calm blue-gray links, readable typography). Tokyo-night inspired dark mode with deep navy canvas.

**Theme**: Light mode primary, dark mode supported (`respectPrefersColorScheme`).

**Color palette**: Warm paper canvas (`#FFF7E6`), pink CTA (`#FF4D6D`), blue-gray reading links (`#4F6D8A` / `#82AAFF`), yellow navbar brand anchor (`#FFD44D` in both modes), ink-black borders (`#111111`). Tokyo-night dark background (`#0F1420`) with cool navy-blue shadows (`#1E2D4A`). Full palette in `keywords/design-tokens.md`.

**Typography**: Space Grotesk (headings, weights 600-700) + Inter (body, weights 400-700) + JetBrains Mono (code). 17px base, 1.75 line height. Headings are editorial and sharp with title case. Reading-optimized spacing throughout.

**References** (what we admire):
- [ccforpms.com](https://ccforpms.com/) — clean educational layout, good design feel
- [profilepicture.ai](https://www.profilepicture.ai/) — polished design and visual feel
- [promptingguide.ai](https://www.promptingguide.ai) — clean docs/guide structure
- [ronit.io](https://ronit.io/) — font choices and color combinations specifically

**Anti-references** (what we must NOT look like):
- News article sites — cluttered, ad-heavy, disposable feel
- Tech blogs — cold, assumes coding knowledge, intimidating
- Academic paper sites — dense, dry, zero visual personality
- Generic "boring reading" sites — walls of text with no visual rhythm or personality
- Anything that makes a non-tech person feel they're in the wrong place

### Design Principles

1. **Reading-first, always** — Every design decision must serve the reading experience. Typography, spacing, and contrast are optimized for 3-7 minute educational articles. If a visual element distracts from reading, remove it.

2. **Bold frame, calm content** — Neobrutalism lives in the structural chrome (navbar, sidebar, footer, cards, buttons) with hard shadows and thick borders. The content area stays warm, spacious, and easy on the eyes. The frame says "this site has personality"; the content area says "settle in and learn."

3. **Physical, not decorative** — Interactive elements feel like physical objects: buttons press down, cards have weight, borders are thick and real. But skip decorative chaos (rotation tilts, floating shapes, retro grids) — this is a learning site, not a landing page.

4. **Yellow is structure, pink is action** — The yellow navbar is the brand anchor in both modes. Pink is reserved for CTAs and conversion moments only. Blue-gray handles links and interactive emphasis. This color discipline keeps the interface controlled.

5. **Accessible without compromise** — Color contrast meets WCAG AA. Color blindness considered in status colors and UI states (never rely on color alone for meaning). Reduced motion respected via `prefers-reduced-motion`. No cognitive overload — one idea per section, generous whitespace, clear hierarchy.

### Technical Constraints

- **Framework**: Docusaurus 3.9.2 with `@docusaurus/preset-classic`
- **Styling**: Vanilla CSS overriding Infima design system variables. No Tailwind, no CSS-in-JS.
- **Theming**: CSS custom properties (`--ifm-*` for Infima, `--neo-*` for custom). All in `src/css/custom.css`.
- **Dark mode**: `[data-theme='dark']` selector, auto-detected from OS preference.
- **Build**: `onBrokenLinks: 'throw'` — builds fail on broken links.
- **Content**: MDX with Docusaurus admonitions (`:::info`, `:::tip`, `:::warning`).
- **No external design libraries**: No Tailwind, shadcn, Chakra, etc. Pure CSS + Infima.

### Color Blindness Considerations

- **Links**: Blue-gray (#4F6D8A) is distinguishable from pink (#FF4D6D) across all common color vision deficiencies (protanopia, deuteranopia, tritanopia)
- **Status colors**: Success green (#22C55E), warning yellow (#F59E0B), danger red (#EF4444), info teal (#14B8A6) — chosen for distinguishability across color vision types
- **Never rely on color alone**: All interactive states use additional cues (borders, shadows, weight changes, underlines) alongside color
- **Admonitions**: Use left-border color + icon + label text, not just background color
