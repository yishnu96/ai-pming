## The Quiet Decades

AI was born in 1956, at a summer workshop at Dartmouth College. A small group of researchers believed machines could think. They were right -- but not for a long, long time.

In the decades that followed, AI made progress that almost nobody outside of labs could see or use. Alan Turing asked in 1950 whether machines could think, proposing a test to measure if a computer could convince a human in conversation. ELIZA, a 1966 chatbot, could mimic a therapist well enough to feel eerily real. Deep Blue beat the world chess champion in 1997. ImageNet, started in 2009, gave researchers a massive database to train image recognition systems. GPT-1, GPT-2, and GPT-3 came and went, mostly noticed by researchers and developers.

Think of it like a library writing incredible books no one knew how to check out. The knowledge existed. The access did not.

Then came the winters. Periods where funding dried up, expectations collapsed, and progress slowed to a crawl. It happened twice -- in the 1970s and late 1980s. AI was overhyped, underdelivered, and lost faith. But the researchers kept building in the background, quietly laying the groundwork for what was coming.

## The Moment Everything Changed

<div style={{position: 'relative'}}>
<Star5 color="#06D6A0" stroke="black" strokeWidth={2} size={36} style={{position: 'absolute', top: '5px', right: '15px'}} />

November 30, 2022. OpenAI released ChatGPT as a free public tool. In five days, it hit one million users. To put that in perspective, Facebook took months to reach that same milestone.
</div>

What made this different from all the AI releases before it?

Two things: a simple interface anyone could use, and real-time conversations that actually made sense. Before this, GPT-3 was powerful -- but it was a tool for developers who knew how to work with APIs and code. ChatGPT removed the barrier. You opened a browser, typed a question, and got an answer that understood context, followed up, and felt natural.

The difference between GPT-3 and ChatGPT is the difference between buying a car engine and buying a car. One requires you to build the vehicle yourself. The other, you just drive.

:::

## The Explosion 2023-2026

Remember scrolling through your favorite apps and seeing "powered by AI" badges everywhere? Not a single app escaped it. Search engines, email clients, photo editors, spreadsheets -- all of them added AI features overnight.

The pace accelerated fast. GPT-4 arrived in March 2023 with reasoning abilities that surprised even its creators. Google launched Bard. Anthropic released Claude and then Claude 3. OpenAI released GPT-4o, a multimodal model that could see, hear, and speak. Billions of dollars poured into AI startups. Governments scrambled to write regulations. AI moved from text-only to images, video, voice, and code. And models started running on phones and laptops instead of massive cloud servers.

It happened so quickly that most people are still catching up. If you feel behind, you are not. Almost everyone is.

## Why This Matters

AI went from a laboratory curiosity to a daily professional tool in roughly three years. That is faster than the internet, faster than smartphones, faster than anything most of us have seen in our careers.

You do not need to have been there from the start. You just need to start now. The people who will benefit most from AI are not the ones who studied it since 2022. They are the ones who decide to learn and use it today. Understanding the history tells you something important: AI is not a passing trend. It has survived decades of doubt, multiple winters, and countless failures. It did not give up. Neither should you.

:::tip Key Takeaway

:::
