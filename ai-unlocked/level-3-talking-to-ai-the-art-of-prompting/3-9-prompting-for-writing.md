# Write Better

You asked AI to "write an email to my team about the deadline." You got back a wall of text that sounds like a corporate robot wrote it. Your team noticed. Your manager noticed. *You* definitely noticed.

Here's the truth: AI can write your emails, reports, summaries, social media posts, and blog drafts — if you tell it **how** to write them. A vague prompt gets you generic slop. A specific prompt gives you something you actually want to send.

This topic covers three things: writing work documents, writing for social and blogs, and one universal template that handles anything.

---

## Work Writing That Actually Works

Emails, reports, and summaries share one problem: AI defaults to sounding like a press release. The fix is always giving it the raw ingredients before asking it to cook.

### Prompting for Emails

Instead of writing a blank email from scratch, give AI your rough thoughts and a clear request.

**Bad prompt:**
> Write an email to my team about the project deadline moving.

**Good prompt:**
> I need to email my team of 12 people about a project deadline that moved from June 15 to June 1. The reason is a client request. I want the tone to be calm and straightforward, not apologetic. Keep it under 100 words. Include a note that we'll discuss details in Friday's meeting.

AI now has: **who the reader is**, **what changed**, **why it changed**, **the tone you want**, and **a concrete constraint**. That's the difference between "Dear Valued Team Members, I hope this message finds you well..." and something you'd actually send.

<Callout type="warning" title="Real Example">
Before AI: "Dear Team, I am writing to inform you with a heavy heart that we must move the deadline..."

After: "Team — the client has moved our deadline from June 15 to June 1. We'll discuss the plan on Friday. Let me know what you need this week. Thanks."

Which would you read?
</Callout>

### Prompting for Reports

Reports are where AI shines, because they follow predictable structures. Tell it the structure up front.

```
Write a quarterly sales report based on this data:
- Q1 revenue: $2.3M (up 15% from Q4)
- New customers: 340
- Churn rate: 3.2%
- Top product: Enterprise plan

Format: Executive summary first (2 paragraphs), then key metrics as a bulleted list, then 3 recommendations. Keep it professional but direct.
```

AI builds a draft you can fact-check and tweak. The time saved is in the formatting — not the thinking. *You* provide the judgment, the numbers, and the recommendations. AI arranges them.

### Prompting for Summaries

The most common AI superpower is turning long documents into readable summaries. But "summarize this" gives you garbage. Be specific about what "summary" means to you.

```
Summarize this 12-page market research report in 5 bullet points. Focus only on: (1) market size estimates, (2) our competitors' moves, (3) any mention of regulatory changes. Ignore everything else.
```

**The trick:** Tell AI what to *exclude*. A summary that includes everything is just a shorter version of the original. A summary with boundaries is actually useful.

## Social Media and Blogs

Social content and blog drafts require a different approach than work emails. Here, your **brand voice** and **audience** matter more than ever.

### Prompting for Social Media Posts

Social platforms reward brevity and hooks. Tell AI which platform, who the audience is, and what you want them to do.

```
Write 3 LinkedIn posts about our company launching a new product. 
- Audience: product managers at mid-size companies
- Tone: confident, slightly casual, not salesy
- Each post: 3-4 short lines, ends with a question to drive engagement
- Product: AI tool that auto-generates project status reports
- Avoid: hype words like "revolutionary," "game-changing," or "cutting-edge"
```

Three variations gives you options. The audience specification keeps it focused. The exclusion list ("Avoid...") is what stops it from sounding like an AI wrote it.

### Prompting for Blog Drafts

Don't ask AI to "write a blog post." Ask it to write a *structured draft* based on your outline.

```
Write a blog post draft using this outline:

H1: [Topic: Why your weekly status meetings are wasting time]
H2: The real cost of status meetings
H2: What high-performing teams do instead
H2: 3 alternatives worth trying

Tone: Like explaining to a colleague over coffee. Include one stat about meeting cost. End with a call-to-action asking readers what they tried. Target: 800 words.
```

**Important:** You own the ideas. AI owns the typing. The difference between a draft you can use and one you delete is whether **your** thinking is in the prompt, or whether you expect the AI to be clever for you.

## The Universal Writing Prompt Template

Everything above follows one structure. Memorize it, tweak it, and use it for any writing task:

```
1. ROLE: You are a [clear professional role]
2. CONTEXT: [What's happening, why it matters]
3. TASK: Write a [format] about [specific topic]
4. DETAILS: [Key facts, data points, specifics]
5. AUDIENCE: Who will read this
6. TONE: [Voice style — casual, formal, direct]
7. FORMAT: [Structure, length, layout requirements]
8. AVOID: [What NOT to include or sound like]
```

### The Template in Action

Here's the email from earlier, using the template:

```
You are a project manager writing to your team.
Context: A client moved our deadline from June 15 to June 1.
Task: Write an email informing the team.
Details: Client requested the change. We'll discuss in Friday's meeting.
Audience: 12 team members who know the project well.
Tone: Calm, direct, not apologetic.
Format: Under 100 words.
Avoid: Corporate jargon, excessive formality, apologies.
```

Here's a LinkedIn post using the same template:

```
You are a product marketing manager.
Context: Our new AI tool auto-generates project status reports.
Task: Write a LinkedIn post announcing it.
Details: Launches next week. Free trial available.
Audience: Product managers at mid-size companies.
Tone: Confident, slightly casual, not salesy.
Format: 3-4 short lines ending with a question.
Avoid: "Revolutionary," "game-changing," "cutting-edge," hype.
```

Same skeleton. Completely different output. That's the power of structure.

<Callout type="info" title="When to Skip the Template">
For quick, low-stakes writing — "rewrite this paragraph" or "check my tone" — just ask directly. The template is for anything that goes to a client, your boss, or the public.
</Callout>

---

:::tip Try This Now

1. **Rewrite the email in your head.** Think of the last email you wrote at work. Paste your rough points into AI using the template above. Compare the AI version to yours.

2. **Take a boring document.** Open any recent report or meeting note. Ask AI: "Rewrite this in plain language. Keep all facts. Cut the fluff." See how much clearer it gets.

3. **Practice the template.** Pick one writing task — a LinkedIn post, a client email, or a project brief. Fill in all 8 lines of the universal template. Paste it. Then edit the output like a human would.

:::
