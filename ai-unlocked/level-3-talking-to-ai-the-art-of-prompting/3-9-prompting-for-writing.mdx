## The Fill-in-Blank Template

Every good writing prompt answers seven questions before the AI types a single word.

| # | Element | What It Means |
|---|---------|---------------|
| 1 | **Persona** | Who should the AI pretend to be? |
| 2 | **Audience** | Who will read the output? |
| 3 | **Purpose** | What should the reader do or know after reading? |
| 4 | **Context** | What background info does the AI need? |
| 5 | **Structure** | What format — email, report, social post? |
| 6 | **Tone & Style** | How should it sound? |
| 7 | **Constraints** | Word count, formatting rules, words to avoid? |

Here is the full template as a fill-in-the-blank:

```
You are a [ROLE] writing for [AUDIENCE].
Your goal is to [PURPOSE].
Use the following context: [CONTEXT].
Produce a [STRUCTURE] with a [TONE] tone, limited to [WORD COUNT] words.
Include headings, bullet points, and a call-to-action.
```

Think of it like ordering food at a restaurant. You do not say "bring me something." You say what cuisine, portion size, dietary needs, and how spicy. The more specific you are, the better the dish.

:::info Think About It
Which of these seven pieces do you usually skip when prompting AI? What does your output lose when that piece is missing?
:::

### What It Looks Like Filled In

> *Prompt:* "You are a senior HR manager writing for new hires in the finance department. Your goal is to welcome them and introduce the onboarding schedule, key contacts, and company culture. Context: onboarding runs for 3 days, main contact is Priya from HR, company values collaboration and transparency. Produce a welcome email with a warm and professional tone, limited to 150 words."

The result is a fully formatted email — ready for HR automation tools. No rewrite needed.

**What it is NOT:** This is not "write me a welcome email." That prompt gives the AI zero direction. It will produce something generic — the same thing it would generate for a tech company, a bakery, or a law firm.

## Emails, Reports, Summaries

The fill-in-the-blank template works for every type of workplace writing. But most people do not want seven fields for a quick email. The shortcut uses four pieces only:

```
Role -> Task -> Context -> Tone & Length
```

### Bad Prompt vs Good Prompt

| Task | What Most People Type | What You Type Now |
|------|----------------------|-------------------|
| **Status Email** | "Write an email about the product launch." | "You are a product manager. Write a brief email to the engineering team announcing a shift to a micro-frontend architecture, explain the benefits (scalability, independent deployments), and outline next steps. Keep it under 200 words, casual tone." |
| **Executive Summary** | "Summarize this report." | "Summarize the attached 10-page market analysis into a 2-paragraph executive brief for senior leadership. Focus on market size, growth rate, and competitive threats. Use formal tone." |
| **Meeting Minutes** | "Write meeting notes from this list." | "Generate meeting minutes from these bullet points: Discussed Q3 budget, approved 5 percent increase. Agreed on timeline for redesign. Format as a table with Action, Owner, Deadline columns." |

The pattern is simple. Each prompt identifies who writes, what the output is, what facts to include, and how it should sound. The AI does not guess. It executes.

## Social Posts, Blogs

The same structure applies. You only change the context and format.

### Social Media Prompts

```
Act as a LinkedIn thought-leader. Create a 3-sentence post
about AI-assisted content creation, emphasizing productivity gains
and inviting comments. Use professional but approachable tone.
```

```
Write a 5-tweet thread explaining how to craft effective AI
prompts for sales copy. Include a hook, bullet-point tips,
and a call-to-action.
```

### Blog Prompts

```
You are a business writer. Write an 800-word blog on
'AI Prompting for Effective Email Communication' aimed at
mid-level managers. Include an intro, three practical steps,
and a conclusion with a call-to-action. Use conversational tone.
```

Each prompt follows the same skeleton. Change the platform, adjust the word count, swap the audience — done.

Real use cases you will see at work: marketing teams generating daily tweet calendars, product managers writing weekly status updates, and HR teams producing onboarding emails with consistent branding.

## Lock It In

### Try This Now

**Exercise 1: The Five-Minute Email Rewrite**

1. Open your sent folder and find an email you wrote this week.
2. Copy the key facts from it into the template:
   ```
   You are a [your role] writing for [recipient].
   Your goal is to [what you needed them to know or do].
   Use the following context: [2-3 key facts].
   Produce an email with a [tone] tone, limited to [word count] words.
   ```
3. Run the prompt in your AI tool.
4. Compare the AI output with your original. Keep the best parts of both.

**Exercise 2: The Cliche Word Ban**

Take any content your AI has written for you. Count how many times words like "cutting-edge," "revolutionary," or "unparalleled" appear.

Re-prompt with: "Do not use the words 'cutting-edge,' 'revolutionary,' 'unparalleled,' 'groundbreaking,' or 'innovative.'"

Watch the quality jump.

**Exercise 3: Social Post From Scratch**

Pick a topic you know well. Fill in this prompt and run it:

```
Act as a [platform] creator. Write a post about [topic].
Key points: [point 1], [point 2], [point 3].
Target length: [word count]. Tone: [your style].
End with a question to spark engagement.
```

Compare the result to posts you normally write.
