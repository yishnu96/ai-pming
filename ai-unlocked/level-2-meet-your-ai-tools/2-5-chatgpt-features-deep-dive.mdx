You now know which ChatGPT model to pick for each task. But there's a whole layer underneath that most people never touch — features that save you time, skip the repetition, and let ChatGPT work alongside you instead of just answering questions.

This article will show you exactly which ChatGPT feature to use when, so you stop fighting the tool and start working with it.

## Memory That Sticks

You've told ChatGPT three times you're a marketing manager. It still asked. Here's how to make it stop.

<div style={{position: 'relative'}}>
<Star1 color="#fbbf24" stroke="black" strokeWidth={2} size={36} style={{position: 'absolute', top: '-10px', right: '20px'}} />

ChatGPT has two separate settings that sound similar but do completely different things. Get them mixed up and you'll wonder why nothing sticks.
</div>

**Memory** is what ChatGPT knows about _you_. Your job title. Your team size. Your preferred tone. You tell it once, and it remembers across every future conversation. You don't need to repeat yourself.

**Custom Instructions** is how you want ChatGPT to _behave_ in every conversation. Want it to always use short paragraphs? Prefer bullet points over walls of text? Ask it to assume you're an experienced professional, not a beginner? That's Custom Instructions.

Two different switches. Same settings page. Set them once, and forget they're there.

Here's where people trip up: Memory stores facts about you. Custom Instructions stores rules for the AI. Memory = "I work in SaaS." Custom Instructions = "Always answer in under 200 words." Both matter. Neither replaces the other.

To set these up: click your name in the bottom-left of ChatGPT, then Customizations. You'll see two panels — one for Memory, one for Custom Instructions. Write what you want there once, and every conversation after that starts with context already loaded.

:::info Think About It
What's the one thing you type at the start of almost every ChatGPT conversation? That's exactly what belongs in your Memory or Custom Instructions.
:::

Now that ChatGPT remembers you, the next question is — what if you need it to be an expert, not just a general assistant?

## GPTs and Plugins

ChatGPT can't do your taxes. But someone built a GPT that can.

The GPT Store has over 50,000 options. Most are garbage. Some are gold. The trick is knowing what a GPT actually is — and what it's not.

<div style={{position: 'relative'}}>
<Star5 color="#06D6A0" stroke="black" strokeWidth={2} size={36} style={{position: 'absolute', top: '-10px', right: '20px'}} />

**A GPT is a specialist consultant.** It's a version of ChatGPT with its own custom instructions, built-in knowledge, and specific skills. Tell it your industry, give it a template, and it acts like an expert in that field. Need a GPT that knows your company's style guide? Done. A GPT that generates social media posts in your brand voice? Also done.
</div>

**A Plugin is a tool add-on.** It doesn't change how ChatGPT behaves. It just gives ChatGPT new powers. Web search. Code interpreter. Image generation. Plugins work inside any conversation, with any model.

The difference matters. A GPT is like hiring a specialist for a specific project — a tax accountant who only does taxes. A Plugin is like giving a general assistant a new tool — handing them a calculator mid-conversation.

Here's what happens when you confuse them: You need a legal contract reviewed, so you attach a "web search plugin" thinking it'll find relevant law. It won't. You need a GPT built for legal work — one trained on contract language and legal frameworks. The plugin just finds websites. The GPT understands structure.

:::info Think About It
Think about a task you do weekly at work. Could a purpose-built GPT handle 80% of it? What would you tell it to always know and always do?
:::

Now, building a GPT takes a few minutes once you know what you're doing. But what if you're writing a document right now, in real time, and don't want to switch between tabs?

## Canvas: Edit Alongside

You've been copy-pasting ChatGPT's answers into Word to edit them. There's a better way.

Canvas is a split-screen workspace inside ChatGPT. The left side is your conversation. The right side is a document you can edit — like Google Docs with an AI co-writer sitting next to you.

<Star9 color="#118AB2" stroke="black" strokeWidth={2} size={36} />

It auto-triggers when ChatGPT's output hits about 10 lines or more. Writing a blog post? Canvas opens. Drafting a project brief? Canvas opens. Getting a short answer to a simple question? No Canvas — just the normal chat.

You can ask ChatGPT to rewrite sections, adjust tone, or add examples — and it changes the document on the right in real time. Version history lets you step back if a rewrite goes sideways.

**What Canvas is NOT:** It's not a standalone word processor. You wouldn't replace Google Docs with it for long-term team collaboration. It's a focused writing and editing session between you and ChatGPT.

:::info Think About It
When was the last time you copy-pasted something out of ChatGPT to edit it elsewhere? That's the exact moment Canvas would have saved you five minutes.
:::

So you've got Memory, GPTs, Plugins, and Canvas. But when do you use which? That's the part nobody explains clearly.

## Which Feature When

Here's the decision map. Three problems, three features:

| What you're dealing with | What to use | Why |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| "I keep repeating myself" | Memory + Custom Instructions | Tell it once, never again |
| "I need industry-specific help" | GPTs | Specialist knowledge baked in |
| "I'm writing a document" | Canvas | Edit side-by-side, no switching |

<div style={{ position: 'relative', marginTop: '12px' }}>
<Star2 color="#FF5757" stroke="black" strokeWidth={2} size={28} style={{ position: 'absolute', top: '-8px', left: '-40px' }} />

**What happens when you use the WRONG feature:**

Use a general GPT when you needed a specialist GPT — you'll get generic advice that sounds right but misses your industry's rules. Use Memory when you needed Canvas — you'll save context but still be stuck copy-pasting to actually edit anything. Use a Plugin when you needed a GPT — you'll get tool access but no expertise.
</div>
