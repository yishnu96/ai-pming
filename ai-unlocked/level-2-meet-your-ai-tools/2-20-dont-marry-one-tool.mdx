## The Mistake Everyone Makes

<div style={{position: 'relative', marginBottom: '1rem'}}>
<Star1 color="#fbbf24" stroke="black" strokeWidth={2} size={36} style={{position: 'absolute', top: '-10px', right: '20px'}} />

You found an AI you like. You're going to use it for everything. Don't.
</div>

Using one AI tool for everything is like using a single app on your phone for every task. Sure, you could try writing notes, setting reminders, and planning trips all inside your camera app. It technically works. But you're leaving a lot of power on the table.

Here's the truth: **every AI tool has blind spots**. One might write great emails but struggle with facts. Another might be brilliant at research but terrible at creative ideas. When you rely on a single tool, you inherit all of its weaknesses.

When you cross-check between two or three tools, something useful happens. They catch each other's mistakes. One fills in what the other misses. That's the difference between "this AI is okay" and "this AI is genuinely helping me."

:::info Think About It
What's one area where your current AI tool keeps falling short? That gap is your reason to look elsewhere.
:::

## Your AI Stack

The concept of an **AI stack** is simple: 3-5 tools you use regularly, each for what it does best.

You don't need all of them. You need the right combination.

**Your primary tool (daily driver)** — The one you open most. It handles 60-70% of what you do. Maybe it writes your drafts, answers quick questions, and helps you think through problems. This is your go-to.

**Your research tool** — The one that checks the internet in real time and gives you sources you can actually verify. When you need current facts, not just what the model was trained on months ago, this is the one to open.

**Your ecosystem tool** — The one already built into the apps you use every day. If you live in Google Docs and Gmail, that's Gemini. If you're in Word and Outlook, that's Copilot. The best AI tool for a task might be the one you don't have to copy-paste into.

:::info Think About It
If you had to drop one of those three roles — primary, research, or ecosystem — which would hurt the most? That tells you what to invest in first.
:::

## Test Without Wasting Time

Try before you buy — the smart way.

Here's a simple way to evaluate any new AI tool without burning hours or money. **Always start with the free tier.** Give it a one-week test: use it for five real work tasks that actually matter (not hypothetical ones). After that week, ask yourself one question: "Did this save me time?"

If the answer is yes, keep it. If it's no or "not really," close the tab. You're not locked in.

And here's a trap — **don't switch every time a new tool launches**. The AI market moves fast. New launches happen weekly. If you chase every one, you'll spend more time learning new interfaces than getting work done. Switch only when your current tool consistently frustrates you on tasks that matter.

## Stay Flexible

Here's the honest truth: the AI tool you love today might be obsolete next year. That's okay. It's supposed to be.

This space moves fast enough that anyone who tells you "this is the one you'll use forever" is either selling you something or guessing. The only constant in AI right now is that it keeps changing.

So don't build your entire workflow around one company. Build it around **your own needs**. Your need for better emails won't change. Your need for faster research won't change. Which tool serves those needs might.

:::info Think About It
If ChatGPT vanished tomorrow, could you take the prompts you've been writing and use them in Claude or Gemini? (The answer is yes — you can start doing exactly that.)

## Try This Now

1. List three tasks you do most days. Things like: writing emails, researching a topic, summarizing a meeting.
2. Match each task to a **different** AI tool. Don't use the same one for all three.
3. This week, use each tool for its matched task. Pay attention to which one felt fastest, which result you trusted most.
4. Next week, keep the winner for that task. Move on.

This is how you build an AI stack. Not by reading reviews. By doing the work and noticing what actually serves it.
